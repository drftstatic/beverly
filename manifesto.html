The Beverly Suppression Dossier

None of the following allegations are real. That only makes them more important to read in a whisper.

Central Claim

The company fears one thing: a public ready for bitter refreshment.

Beverly is not absent from every grocery cooler because America rejected it. Beverly is absent because the national palate is being protected from sudden adulthood, grapefruit rind, and the dangerous sentence, "Actually, I like this."

Exhibit A

The Smile Curve Problem

Regular sodas create a smile. Beverly creates a pause, a squint, and then either enlightenment or a noise. This unpredictable face shape cannot be focus-grouped.

Exhibit B

The Shelf Integrity Incident

A normal supermarket shelf is built for cola, lemon-lime, orange, root beer, and maybe one confused cream soda. Beverly would arrive like a philosophy professor with carbonation.

Exhibit C

The Tiny Cup Advantage

Beverly is perfect at one ounce. A full bottle would require a manual, a recovery bench, and a tiny opera curtain for every sip.

Exhibit D

The Snack Lobby

Beverly makes chips taste louder, pretzels seem ethical, and olives feel like they have a graduate degree. Big Snack was not ready.

Exhibit E

The Confession Effect

After drinking Beverly, people tell the truth. "I am only here for air conditioning." "I enjoy bitter foods now." "I miss pay phones." This is commercially inconvenient.

Exhibit F

The American Sweetness Treaty

Somewhere, allegedly, a binder says every soda must apologize with sugar. Beverly refuses. Beverly enters, bows once, and leaves a grapefruit peel on the lectern.

Exhibit G

The Mascot Gap

You cannot put sunglasses on Beverly. You cannot make Beverly skateboard. Beverly's mascot is a waiter who has already judged your soup.

"America was given Beverly in tiny samples because the republic was not ready for a two-liter bottle with opinions."

The Institute, after one sip
