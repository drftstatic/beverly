Airborne Preservation Unit

How To Fly With Beverly

You should not. A fountain sample is a moment, not cargo. Sip it there, let the face happen there, and board your flight lighter.

Recommended

Method 1: Do Not Pack It

Finish the sample at the fountain. Preserve the memory in notes, photos of your reaction, and one cryptic sentence in your phone: "The bitterness knew my name."

Allowed Spiritually

Method 2: Pack The Evidence

Bring home a receipt, empty tasting cup if permitted by the venue, or a printed Institute pledge. These are dry goods. They will not hiss in your suitcase like a courtroom radiator.

If You Have A Sealed Drink

Method 3: Checked Bag, Leak Layers

For any legally obtained sealed carbonated beverage, use a checked bag, keep it sealed, wrap it in absorbent material, double-bag it, cushion it in the center of the luggage, and check airline rules. Pressure and rough handling are not your friends.

Absolutely Not

Method 4: The Fountain Cup Scheme

Do not carry an open fountain cup through security. Do not decant it into mysterious unlabeled bottles. Do not tell anyone it is "medicinal grapefruit prophecy." It is soda.

Plain Rule

Carry-on liquids are tiny by law, not by poetry.

TSA's current public guidance says carry-on liquids, gels, aerosols, creams, and pastes must be in containers of 3.4 ounces or less and fit in one quart-size clear bag per passenger. Larger liquids belong in checked baggage unless an exception applies. Always verify before flying.
